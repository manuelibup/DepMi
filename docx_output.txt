1. The Deps System — Social Currency & Credibility
"Deps" is one of the most creative UX ideas for this platform. It transforms DepMi from just another marketplace into a gamified, trust-driven community where every transaction builds your reputation. Here’s the full system design:
1.1 What Is a Dep?
A Dep is earned every time a user completes a transaction on the platform — either as a buyer or a seller. One completed purchase = 1 Dep. One completed sale = 1 Dep. It’s a combined credibility score that reflects how active and trusted you are.
Example
Dara has 150 Deps. This means she’s completed 150 transactions total — maybe 90 sales and 60 purchases. A buyer seeing "150 Deps" instantly knows Dara is active, trusted, and experienced. It’s a reputation score you can’t fake because it’s backed by real completed transactions, not just follows.
1.2 My Recommendation: Use BOTH Systems Together
You had two great ideas: (A) Deps as a single credibility number, and (B) Buyers/Sellers as your follow system. My strong recommendation: use both. They serve completely different purposes, and together they create a profile system no competitor has. Here’s how:

3. Payment Architecture & DepMi Wallet
This is the most important section of the entire addendum. Payments are the lifeblood of the platform, and how you handle money determines everything: your revenue, regulatory obligations, user trust, and scalability. Let me explain exactly how it works.
3.1 How the 2% Fee Actually Works
Great question. The money never goes "straight to the seller." Instead, DepMi sits in the middle of every transaction. Here’s the exact flow:
Payment Flow (Without DepMi Wallet)
1. Buyer pays ₦10,000 for a product.
2. Payment goes to DEPMI’S Paystack/Flutterwave account (not the seller’s).
3. DepMi’s system calculates: ₦10,000 × 2% = ₦200 platform fee.
4. DepMi keeps ₦200 in its own account.
5. DepMi transfers ₦9,800 to the seller’s linked bank account.

This is called "split payment" or "marketplace settlement." Paystack has a built-in feature for this called Transaction Splits that handles it automatically.
Option A: Paystack Transaction Splits (Simplest — Recommended for MVP)
Paystack’s Transaction Splits feature was built specifically for marketplaces like DepMi. Here’s how it works:
You create a "split configuration" in Paystack that says: "For every transaction, keep 2% for DepMi, send 98% to the seller’s subaccount."
Each seller on DepMi has a Paystack Subaccount linked to their bank account (BVN-verified).
When a buyer pays, Paystack automatically splits the money: 2% goes to DepMi’s main Paystack account, 98% goes to the seller’s subaccount.
Paystack settles both parties automatically (typically T+1, meaning next business day).
You never touch the seller’s money directly. Paystack handles the split, settlement, and compliance.
Paystack charges 1.5% + ₦100 per local transaction (waived under ₦2,500). This fee is separate from your 2%. So the real math on a ₦10,000 order is:

Flutterwave offers a similar marketplace split feature. The main difference: Flutterwave charges 1.4% per local transaction (slightly cheaper) and supports more African countries. If you plan to expand to Ghana, Kenya, or South Africa early, Flutterwave is worth considering alongside Paystack.
3.2 The DepMi Wallet — Yes, But With a Critical Warning
Having an in-app wallet is absolutely possible and is a powerful feature. OPay, PalmPay, and every major Nigerian fintech has one. But there’s a massive regulatory catch you need to understand:
CRITICAL: CBN Licensing Requirement
To hold customer funds in a wallet, you MUST have a Mobile Money Operator (MMO) license from the Central Bank of Nigeria. This requires:

• ₦2 billion in paid-up share capital (~$1.28M)
• ₦2 billion escrow deposit with the CBN (~$1.28M)
• 8–24 months licensing process
• Ongoing compliance: monthly CBN reports, KYC enforcement, AML monitoring, annual audits

Paystack was recently fined ₦250 million by the CBN for launching Zap (a consumer wallet product) without the proper license. Operating a wallet without a license is not just risky — it can shut down your entire business.

This does NOT mean you can’t have a wallet. It means you need a strategy.

3.3 How the DepMi Wallet Works (Phase 2+)

• DepMi earns: ₦200 (2% platform fee)

4.3 Product Request Posts — The Demand Engine
This feature deserves special attention because it’s genuinely unique. No major platform does this well. Here’s the design:
How Product Request Posts Work

5.3 Why NOT to Build DMs for MVP
Building a real-time messaging system is a significant engineering undertaking. Here’s what it requires:
WebSocket infrastructure: Persistent connections for real-time delivery. Need a dedicated service (Socket.io, Pusher, or Ably). Adds cost and complexity.
Message storage: Potentially millions of messages. Need efficient database design (separate message tables, pagination, indexing).
Push notifications: Firebase Cloud Messaging (Android) + APNs (iOS) integration for offline message delivery.
Media handling: Image upload, compression, storage (S3/Cloudinary), and delivery in chat bubbles.
Moderation: Spam detection, reporting, blocking. Without moderation, DMs become a spam vector on day one.
Encryption: Users expect private messages to be secure. End-to-end encryption is ideal but complex.
Estimated development time: 4–6 weeks for a basic DM system, 8–12 weeks for a polished one. That’s time better spent on core commerce features in the MVP. Comments + WhatsApp links give you 90% of the communication value at 10% of the cost.

5. Pros & Cons of Building This
5.1 Pros (Why This Can Work)
Massive Market: 41M+ MSMEs in Nigeria, $8.5B e-commerce market growing at 12% CAGR. Social commerce specifically is set to double by 2030.
Real Pain Point: Vendors genuinely struggle with scattered operations across 4-5 apps. The “one place for everything” pitch resonates deeply.
Mobile-First Advantage: 82% of Nigerian e-commerce is mobile. Building mobile-first (not desktop-first) aligns perfectly with user behavior.
WhatsApp Integration Moat: 90M+ WhatsApp users in Nigeria. A store that updates via WhatsApp DM removes the biggest friction point for non-technical vendors.
Network Effects: Social features create network effects that pure commerce platforms don’t have. More vendors attract more buyers, attract more vendors.
Low Competition for Full Stack: Bumpa is the closest competitor but lacks social features, AI, and community. There’s a clear gap for a full-stack social commerce platform.
Cultural Fit: Agent/reseller commerce, community-based trust, WhatsApp-first communication — this platform is designed for how Nigerians actually do business.
Revenue Diversity: Transaction fees, premium subscriptions, promoted listings, API access, agent commissions. Multiple monetization paths.
5.2 Cons (Risks & Challenges)
Logistics Nightmare: Nigeria’s delivery infrastructure is unreliable. You’ll need to either build logistics partnerships or stay platform-only (let sellers handle delivery). This is the #1 challenge in Nigerian e-commerce.
Payment Complexity: Integrating Stripe + Paystack + Flutterwave + OPay + PalmPay + crypto is technically complex and requires compliance across multiple regulatory frameworks (CBN, SEC).
Trust Deficit: Nigerian consumers have been burned by online scams. Building trust requires verification systems, escrow, reviews, and potentially KYC — all of which add cost and friction.
Naira Volatility: Currency fluctuations make multi-currency pricing and crypto integration complex. You’ll need real-time FX rate management.
Chicken-and-Egg Problem: You need vendors to attract buyers, but vendors won’t come without buyers. This is the classic marketplace cold-start problem.
Technical Complexity: AI visual search, WhatsApp chatbot integration, real-time analytics, multi-payment gateway — this is a LOT of engineering. You’ll need to phase it carefully.
Internet Infrastructure: Variable internet speeds across Nigeria mean the platform must be lightweight, work on 2G/3G, and have offline capabilities.
Competition Response: Bumpa has $4.2M in funding and Meta partnerships. They could add social features. Jumia could add community features. Speed to market matters.
Regulatory Risk: Nigerian fintech regulation is evolving rapidly. Crypto payments in particular face regulatory uncertainty from the CBN.
